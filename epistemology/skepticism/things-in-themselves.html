Do we experience objects out in the world?

Variation in perspective

Shapes

If I were to stand directly over some table, I will report that it is a rectangle. But if you stand off to the side of that same table, you will report that it looks slightly trapezoidal.

But, no object can be both rectangluar and trapezoidal. So, what we see isn’t the table itself.

Hot and cold

If one of my hands is very cold and the other very hot, when I place each in a bucket of warm water, I will have two very different sensations.

But no object can be both hot and cold. So, what I feel isn’t the water itself.

Inference

These examples suggest that what we perceive is not the physical objects themselves. Instead, the content of our perceptions seem more accurately to be qualities (colors, shapes, smells).

Then we (almost unconsciously) draw an inference that there are objects out in the world that correspond to our sense-data.

Two worlds

Phenomena

This is the world of our experience. It is the colors, sounds, shapes, and feelings that appear to us when we are awake and attentive.

Neumena

This is the world behind the experiences. It is the reality of objects independent of our interaction with them.

The limit of knowledge

Receptivity: Human knowledge depends on sensibility, and sensibility is receptive: we can have knowledge of an object only in so far as it affects us.

Objects in themselves do not affect us. We are only connected to the phenomenal world.

"Properties that belong to things as they are in themselves can never be given to us through the senses... It is not that through sensibility we are acquainted in a merely confused way with the nature of things as they are in themselves; we are not acquainted with that nature in any way at all." (Kant, quoted by Langton, p. 341)

We know that the objects must exist in order to give us our phenomenal experience, but we can't know anything about them because they do not directly influence us.

How do we know who the murderer is?

By being able to pick him out from all other possible suspects.

How do we know an object in itself?

By being able to single it out from all other possible objects.

We only have direct access to (knowledge of) relational properties of things -- how those things affect us.

But these properties tell us about the thing in itself by helping us distinguish it from all other things.

A new limit to knowledge

Relational properties always make reference to some other objects. Thus, all of our knowledge requires us to have knowledge of other objects, which in turn requires knowlege of further objects.

But this can't go on forever. Eventually, we'll come to objects we can't relate to any further objects. We call these the fundamental objects.

We can't break through to the fundamental objects themselves because we can't signify those objects apart from all other possible objects. The fundamental objects cannot be known to us in themselves.
