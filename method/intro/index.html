Course overview and outcomes

This is an introductory course in philosophy, the primary goals of which are:
  • to provide a broad base of familiarity with areas of concern in academic philosophy; this includes classic philosophical theories and arguments, as well as the original texts in which they are found. Upon completion of the course, students will be able to engage knowledgeably in debates pertaining to a wide variety of philosophical subdisciplines.
  • to develop skills in philosophical argumentation and discussion. Throughout the course, students will critically examine controversial issues, learn how to construct and evaluate logical arguments, and gain experience in presenting their own ideas in rational and thoughtful ways.
  • to connect philosophical theories to real-world issues. By actively engaging in the course, students will come to understand how the ideas we discuss in class matter for their interaction with the world they inhabit.

The course content is divided into 5 modules, each of which reflects an important sub-discipline of philosophy. The modules are: Philosophical method, Epistemology, Mind and metaphysics, Ethics, and Political philosophy.

We will spend roughly equal time on each of these modules, and part of each module will be dedicated to explaining exactly what each subdivision of philosophy amounts to. See the course schedule on Canvas for details.

Attendance, preparation, and participation

Active participation in class discussion is in the best interests of all students. It is the best way to develop an understanding of the course material, and it helps to clear up confusions. Often a question you have is shared by others; by engaging with each other, we mutually improve our understanding.

Exams will draw heavily on material discussed in lectures, and class discussion will go beyond the lecture notes provided on Canvas. In addition, active participation will reflect favorably on course grades in borderline cases. This doesn't mean that you must have something to say for each class, but you should regularly try to contribute.

Participating helpfully requires preparation. Students are expected to read the assigned texts before the class in which they will be addressed.

Frequent absence and tardiness disrupt class discussion and indicate an unwillingness on the part of the student to actively engage in the class. Please show up to class on time. If you must miss a class, please contact me ahead of time with a valid reason for your absence.

Late work and tardiness

Students are expected to submit all assignments by the posted due date and time. Late assignments will be subject to a penalty of one-half letter grade for every day they are past due, unless arrangements are made with the instructor well before the assignment due date.

Exceptions will be made for properly documented emergencies; in these rare instances, students will be given extra time to complete the missed assignment.

Student evaluations

The MSU Philosophy Department collects student evaluations of each course near the end of the semester. These evaluations are greatly useful to the instructor and the department. To ensure high participation in the evaluations, students will be required to email the instructor with a confirmation validation received upon completing the online survey for the course.

Reading Responses

Exams

This class will have two midterm exams and one exam during the finals period. The exams are equally weighted, and each exam will cover approximately one third of the course material. That is to say, the exams will not be cummulative; though, certain concepts covered early in the class will be relevant to later material as well.

The purpose of the exams is for the student to demonstrate an understanding of the philosophical concepts, methods, and arguments introduced in course lectures.

Format

Each exam is worth 100 points and has two parts:

  1. Short answer questions (40 points)

    • The short answer questions will test your ability to recall the philosophical concepts introduced in class.

    • There will be some fill in the blank questions as well as questions that ask you to state particular principles of premises of interest.

    • Complete responses to these questions should require no more than one paragraph.

  2. Extended response questions (60 points)

    • The extended answer questions will test your comprehension of the philosophical discussions covered in class.

    • You will be expected to explain clearly and succinctly certain philosophical arguments, positions, and principles.

    • You will be given a choice of multiple prompts (between 3 and 6) from which you will choose a selection to answer (between 2 and 4).

    • It is difficult to give a definitive length for these responses. The most accurate advice I can give is that you should answer the question completely. A more informative answer is that the response should be at least a paragraph, and likely two or three in most cases.

Grading

The grade for each of your answers will be determined by the accuracy, completeness, and clarity of your responses, with these components roughly equally weighted.

  • A response that fails to address material relevant to the question will be docked for its incompleteness.

  • A response that misconstrues the material or includes excessive material not relevant to the question will be docked for its inaccuracy.

  • A response that is hard to reas or to follow, or a response that appears to be a mere regurgitation of various keywords from the notes will be docked for its unclarity.

Reading responses

Critical reading of texts is an invaluable skill in philosophical investigation. The reading responses are designed to help you hone this skill. Thus, you can think of reading responses as akin to lab reports in an introductory science class.

Format

  • Reading responses are short writing assignments. They should be 2-3 pages (double spaced, 11-12pt font, 1in margins) and have two parts:

    1. Exegesis: A summary of a portion of the reading under discussion, including argument premises, key theses, and significant implications. This part is intended for you to display your reading comprehension; the summary should be in your own words, and direct quotation should be used only sparingly.

    2. Critique: An analysis of the philosophical content of the reading under discussion. The nature of this section is fairly open ended. Some options include: a development of a critique of the author’s thesis, an alternative to the author’s proposal, an application of the author’s proposal to an aspect of daily life, a comparison of the theory to alternate theories discussed in class.

  • You should dedicate approximately equal space to each part.

  • The frst page of your response should include your name, the title of the article under discussion, and the assignment due date.

  • Reading responses should be saved in PDF format and submitted via Canvas by noon on the appropriate due date. Almost all current word processing programs allow you to export files as PDF. If you are uncertain how to do this, please take the time to learn well before the first assignment due date.

Grading

Each reading response is worth 40 points. The exegesis and critique are each worth 15 points, with the remaining 10 points for the overall clarity and cohesion of the response.

There are 6 reading response assignments throughout the semester, one for each module covered. Each response must address one of the readings from the relevant module. Your final grade will only include 5 responses, and I will throw out your lowest response grade.

The grading criteria for each section of the response are as follows, with the criteria roughly equally weighted:

  • Exegesis

    • Accuracy: The exegesis should accurately portray the author’s position.

    • Focus: One cannot possibly summarize an entire philosophy paper in 1.5 pages. It is important to focus one’s discussion on a specific portion of the reading.

    • Detail: In philosophical writing, the method one uses and the arguments one employs are just as important as the conclusion one goes for. It is important to present the details of the portion of the reading you focus on, and not just the author’s main conclusion.

  • Critique

    • Originality: You are not expected to revolutionize the field of philosophy, but your critique should reflect your own thinking on the issue. You can make use of class lectures and the introductions in the textbook in forming your ideas, but be sure to acknowledge any sources you use.

    • Relevance: The critique should address the issues presented in the exegesis.

    • Specificity: When offering a critique, it is important to be clear on just what issue one is addressing. Broad generalizations can be provacative, but it is attention to the specific issues that makes a critique compelling.

One's policy for life

"My philosophy of life is YOLO"

A way of approaching thought

"She's a very philosophical person. She always thinks very deeply about things."

An academic discipline

"The philosophy department is located in Dickson Hall."

Philosophy as an academic discipline

Topic of study

  • Epistemology investigates the nature of knowledge
  • Metaphysics investigates the structure of the world
  • Ethics investigates the nature of morality and how to behave

Goal of engaging in the study

  • To improve one's understanding of the world
  • To come to terms with the human condition
  • To look smart and impress your friends

Method of undertaking the study

  • Provide reasons
  • Offer arguments
  • Analyze concepts

How does philosophy improve our understanding?

Option 1: Art as a model

  • Art can be a source of profound insight. Individuals can access the sublime through engaging with art.
  • An artist can improve understanding through metaphor and imagery.

Application to philosophy

  • Good philosophy can also be a source of profound insight.
  • But artistic insight is that it is often very fleeting and personal experience.
  • Philosophy aims to provide more lasting and objective understanding.

Option 2: Science as a model

  • Science improves our understanding of the world by discovering new facts about the way the world works.
  • When the scientist's work is accepted, we have a broader understanding of the world.

Application to philosophy

  • But this method of progress doesn't work for philosophy.
  • Philosophy rarely results in one set of facts being universally accepted.
  • We will discuss many debates that originated long ago but are still alive today.

Option 3: Depth of understanding

  • But philosophy can make progress! It does so by:
  • challenging preconceived notions about the way the world works,
  • uncovering nuance in our concepts of the world, and
  • requiring us to defend our beliefs.
