Reason as philosophical method

Last class, we suggested that the value of philosophy comes in providing depth of understanding

One's understanding is shallow when they merely believe something without reflection or consideration of why they believe it.

One's understanding becomes deeper when they acquire some reason for holding their belief.

It is even deeper when they can share that reason with others.

An important element of philosophical method involves the giving and receiving reasons for belief.

The word reason can be used in many different ways

To help get a grip on what it means to engage in the practice of giving and receiving reasons for beliefs, let's examine some of the different kinds of reasons we might profer for a position.

Inquiry

  • Think of the sum total of an individual’s beliefs as determining a picture of the world.

  • An inquiry is any process of forming, testing, and revising one’s picture.

  • If all goes well, at the end of inquiry, one has come to hold a more accurate picture of the world.

  • Inquiry can be an individual process, where one person investigates a part of the world to directly increase her knowledge.

  • But commonly, it is done via communicative exchange with other inquirers.

Communicative exchange

  • Initial state
  • Goal
  • Procedure

Initial state

We all enter any communicative exchange with certain presuppositions (things that we take for granted, our current picture of the world) and uncertainties (questions that we want to get resolved).

Goal

To resolve our uncertainties and revise our presuppositions in a way that provides us with a more accurate picture of the world as a whole.

Procedure

  • An honest, cooperative exchange of reasons for our beliefs.

  • This means offering what we take to be good reasons for our own beliefs, and honestly assessing the reasons that others offer.

  • When we either accept or reject the beliefs of another inquirer, we want it to be on the basis of the reasons brought to bear in the course of the inquiry.

Explanatory v. Justificatory

One role that reasons play is that they serve as answers to Why? questions. But Why? questions can be ambiguous.

Marble rye

Why did Frank take the marble rye home?

  1. Because he paid for it.
  2. Because he thought no one was looking.

Imagine that 1 is true

Then it provides a reason for why Frank took the marble rye, and supposing that having paid for something gives someone the right to do with it what they please, it is a reason that justifies Frank’s behavior.

Suppose that 2 is true (and 1 is false)

Then, this also provides a reason for Frank’s behavior, but it doesn’t seem to justify that behavior. Instead, one might offer this reason as a way of explaining why Frank did it.

Subjective v. objective

Good reasons aren't always available to us, even when we are being fully rational.

Our goal as inquirers is to come to mutually accept objective reasons. But if someone’s behavior doesn’t make sense from our perspective, it can be valuable to see if we can make sense of it in terms of the subjective reasons they have at their disposal.

Gasoline

Imagine that you are thirsty. In front of you is a glass that is full of a clear liquid. The liquid appears to be water. I know (but you don’t) that the liquid is gasoline.

In this scenario, both statements below can be true:

There is reason for you to drink the liquid in the glass.

This claim references a subjective reason. Given the internal, personal state of your knowledge (and your desire to quench your thirst), it makes sense for you to drink the liquid.

There is reason for you to not drink the liquid in the glass.

This references an objective reason. Given the state of the world as it actually is (and your desire to not die), it doesn’t make sense for you to drink the liquid.

Pro tanto v. On balance

Something can be a reason for some belief when viewed on its own, but when viewed in a broader context that includes other reasons, it gets outweighed or made irrelevant.

The investigation

Sherlock Holmes is investigating a crime scene. He has two suspects in the crime, Dirk and Sally.

The blond hair

He first comes upon a blond hair. He knows that Sally has blond hair, whereas Dirk is a brunette. This leads Holmes to say:

"I now have reason to believe that Sally is the culprit."

The fingerprint

As he investigates further, he finds a solid fingerprint. His friend at the police station tells him that there is no strong correlation with the fingerprint of Sally’s that they have on file. Since fingerprint evidence is more weighty than hair folicles, Holmes now says:

Now the balance of reasons suggests that Sally did not do it.

The hair dye

More searching uncovers a bottle of brown hair dye in the trash and a man’s footprint at the scene. Having completed a thorough search, Holme’s exclaims:

Aha! It wasn’t Sally at all, but Dirk!

The analysis

In this example, Holmes' first statement seems accurate at first blush. It is a good reason to believe that Sally did it, as far as it goes. We give this kind of reason the Latin title pro tanto.

But two things happen as Holmes looks into the case in more detail.

He gets rebutting evidence against the hair blond hair

The fingerprint evidence stands in direct conflict with what the hair seems to show. And the fingerprint outweighs, or rebutts the hair.

He gets undercutting evidence against the hair

When he discovers the hair dye, he realizes that a blond hair doesn’t support Sally’s guilt at all, since Dirk very well could have been blond, and then dyed his hair as a disguise. Thus, the dye undercuts the hair.

Finally, having put all the pieces together, Holmes forms an on balance opinion that Dirk is the criminal.

Goals of inquiry

"There are two ways of looking at our duty in the matter of opinion... We must know the truth; and we must avoid error, -- these are our fist and great commandments as would-be knowlers; but they are not two ways of stating an identical commandment, they are two separable laws... and by choosing between them we may end by coloring differently our whole intellectual life." (pp. 80-81)

Matters of choice

Live

A live option is a proposition for which both its truth and its falsity are open to us. It is not settled one way or the other.

Forced

A forced option is one for which we must make a decision as to its truth. We cannot remain agnostic.

"Scepticism... is not a avoidance of option; it is option of a certain particular kind of risk." (p. 83)

Momentous

A momentous option is one for which the decision bears greatly on our lives. Perhaps the risk is great, or the opportunity rare.

"There are... cases where a fact cannot come unless a preliminary faith exists in its coming. And where faith in a fact can help create the fact, that would be an insane logic which should say that faith running ahead of scientific evidence is the 'lowest kind of immorality' into which a thinking thing can fall."

Epistemic vs. pragmatic reasons

Reasons speak in favor of some belief. But there are different ways that a belief can have something going for it.

The will to believe

  • Bear chase
  • Don't believe
  • Believe
  • Ambiguity

Bear chase

You are being chased through the woods by a bear. All of a sudden, you break into a clearing and approach the edge of a chasm. The chasm looks to be about 20ft wide. It occurs to you that if you try to change course, you will most certainly be devoured by the bear. But you also recall that the best long jumpers in the world rarely jump more than 25ft, and you are no world class athlete. You are in quite a predicament.

You have reason to believe that you cannot make the jump.

All of your evidence suggests that the distance is too far for you to cross in a single leap.

You have reason to believe that you can make the jump.

If you don’t jump, you’re gonna get eaten. And maybe believing you can make it will give you a slightly better chance of doing so. So, you might as well believe it.

Different kinds of belief

On their face, these claims and are in direct contradition. The fact that they both seem true suggests that there is an ambiguity in our use of the word “reason”.

Let’s use the term epistemic reason to refer to the kind of reason mentioned in 1. Epistemic reasons appeal to our cognitive faculties and purport to support the truth of some claim.

And let’s call the kind of reason mentioned in 2, pragmatic reason. Pragmatic reasons appeal to our connative faculties (our senses of emotion and well-being) and purport to show that we benefit in some way from believing the claim.

Reasons as support

Reasons provide support for beliefs, giving the belief a ground on which it is based.

The support relation is what structures one's system of beliefs, or their picture of the world.

Thus, we can think of one's reasons as the glue that holds one's picture of the world together. But there are different ways to understand the support relation.

Foundationalism

Some of our beliefs rest upon other of our beliefs. Those, in turn, rest on other beliefs. And so on, until we reach a set of beliefs that are bedrock. We believe them without any other support, and our entire belief system rest upon them.

Coherentism

Though our beliefs rest upon others for support, we never reach a bedrock. Instead, our beliefs mutually support eachother. Our belief system holds itslef together, and it floats above and aprat from the world.
