Pro tanto v. On balance

Something can be a reason for some belief when viewed on its own, but when viewed in a broader context that includes other reasons, it gets outweighed or made irrelevant.

The investigation

Sherlock Holmes is investigating a crime scene. He has two suspects in the crime, Dirk and Sally.

The blond hair

He first comes upon a blond hair. He knows that Sally has blond hair, whereas Dirk is a brunette. This leads Holmes to say:

"I now have reason to believe that Sally is the culprit."

The fingerprint

As he investigates further, he finds a solid fingerprint. His friend at the police station tells him that there is no strong correlation with the fingerprint of Sally’s that they have on file. Since fingerprint evidence is more weighty than hair folicles, Holmes now says:

Now the balance of reasons suggests that Sally did not do it.

The hair dye

More searching uncovers a bottle of brown hair dye in the trash and a man’s footprint at the scene. Having completed a thorough search, Holme’s exclaims:

Aha! It wasn’t Sally at all, but Dirk!

The analysis

In this example, Holmes' first statement seems accurate at first blush. It is a good reason to believe that Sally did it, as far as it goes. We give this kind of reason the Latin title pro tanto.

But two things happen as Holmes looks into the case in more detail.

He gets rebutting evidence against the hair blond hair

The fingerprint evidence stands in direct conflict with what the hair seems to show. And the fingerprint outweighs, or rebutts the hair.

He gets undercutting evidence against the hair

When he discovers the hair dye, he realizes that a blond hair doesn’t support Sally’s guilt at all, since Dirk very well could have been blond, and then dyed his hair as a disguise. Thus, the dye undercuts the hair.

Finally, having put all the pieces together, Holmes forms an on balance opinion that Dirk is the criminal.
