Kinds of arguments

We can distinguish arguments based on the nature of the support the premises are meant to provide for the conclusion

Deductive

In a deductive argument, the premises provide conclusive support for the conclusion. If the premises are actually true, then there's no way the conclusion can be false.

  1. John is taller than Bill.

  2. Bill is taller than George.

  3. So, John is taller than George.

Inductive

In an inductive argument, the premises make it more likely that the conclusion is true. But they don't guarantee that the conclusion is true.

  1. Most people who enjoy tofu are vegetarians.

  2. Frank enjoys tofu.

  3. So, Frank is probably a vegetarian.

Abductive

In an abductive argument, the premises provide a good explanation of why the conclusion is true. But even a good explanation can sometimes be incorrect.

  1. Every time there is a lunar eclipse, the Earth’s shadow appears round.

  2. If the Earth is a sphere, it would make sense that it’s shadow is always round.

  3. So, the Earth must be a sphere.
