Arguments as reasons

In a communicative exchange, our objective is to provide reasons for the beliefs we hold. In order to do this, we need a way of conveying our reasons.

The most important tool that philosopher's have for conveying reasons is the argument.

In this class, we’re going to try to put all our reasons that we give for various beliefs into the form of an argument.

The purpose of this practice is to make it clear what exactly we are offering as our reason, and also to make it easier to assess whether our reason is a good one.

Frequently when we start an inquiry, we may not have well worked out arguments. Instead, we look for what we might call considerations. Considerations are ideas that seem to speak in favor of one conclusion over another.

Once we get a bunch of considerations on the table, we can begin sorting through them and develop them into arguments to see whether the balance of considerations does support one conclusion over the other and how strongly.

But what exactly is an argument?

Basic structure of arguments

A philosophical argument is...

A set of propositions

Propositions are statements that make some claim about the world.

One of these is labeled the conclusion.

The rest are called the premises.

And a relation among them

The premises are said to support the conclusion.

We can also say that the conclusion is believed as an inference from the premises.

In order to provide a complete reason in favor of your conclusion, you need to provide both the supporting premises and the nature of the support those premises provide for the conclusion. When you’ve done that, you’ve given your reasoning in favor of the conclusion.

Kinds of arguments

We can distinguish arguments based on the nature of the support the premises are meant to provide for the conclusion

Deductive

In a deductive argument, the premises provide conclusive support for the conclusion. If the premises are actually true, then there's no way the conclusion can be false.

  1. John is taller than Bill.

  2. Bill is taller than George.

  3. So, John is taller than George.

Inductive

In an inductive argument, the premises make it more likely that the conclusion is true. But they don't guarantee that the conclusion is true.

  1. Most people who enjoy tofu are vegetarians.

  2. Frank enjoys tofu.

  3. So, Frank is probably a vegetarian.

Abductive

In an abductive argument, the premises provide a good explanation of why the conclusion is true. But even a good explanation can sometimes be incorrect.

  1. Every time there is a lunar eclipse, the Earth’s shadow appears round.

  2. If the Earth is a sphere, it would make sense that it’s shadow is always round.

  3. So, the Earth must be a sphere.

Assessing arguments

  • Deductive
  • Inductive

Deductive arguments

Validity

Validity measures the support relation

Deductive arguments purport to give conclusive reason to believe the conclusion.

If the argument is a good one, the conclusion follows directly from the premises.

We say that a deductive argument is valid just in case if the premises are all true, then the conclusion must be true.

Soundness

Soundness tracks the truth of the premises

But not all valid arguments have true premises.

If a valid argument also has true premises, we say that it is sound.

If we have a sound argument, we know for certain that the conclusion is true.

Examples

Invalid argument

  1. All penguins are birds. (True)

  2. Some birds fly. (True)

  3. Therefore , penguins fly. (False)

Valid but unsound argument

  1. Either Eli Manning is a running back or dolphins are fish. (False)

  2. Dolphins are not fish. (True)

  3. So , Eli Manning is a running back. (False)

Inductive arguments

Strength

Strength (measures the support relation)

Inductive arguments purport to give reason to believe that the conclusion is probable.

The better the argument is, the more probable the conclusion becomes.

We say that an inductive argument is strong just in case if the premises are all true, then the conclusion is very likely to be true.

Cogency

Cogency (measures the truth of the premises)

But not all strong arguments have true premises.

If a strong argument also has true premises, we say that it is cogent.

If we have a cogent argument, we know that the conclusion very probably true.

Examples

Weak argument

  1. I know a lawyer who is a total liar. (True)

  2. Bill is also a lawyer. (True)

  3. Consequently , Bill is probably a liar. (False)

Strong but not cogent argument

  1. 89% of male college basketball players are over 6 feet tall. (False)

  2. Bill is a college basketball player. (True)

  3. Thus , Bill is probably over 6 feet tall. (True)

Working through an argument

Authors don't always lay their argument out in a neat fashion for you. You may have to do some work in bringing the argument out.

First, try to determine the author's primary conclusion.

Then look for major premises that the author explicitly mentions in favor of the conclusion.

But don't assume that the author will explicitly say everything that is important to the argument. They may leave some premises implicit. It is important to look for these and add them to your argument structure.

Once you have all the premises in place, make sure you understand the reasoning involved in getting from the premises to the conclusion.

Then, you can start to evaluate the argument.

  • Critique the reasoning: validity/strength
  • Refute a premise: soundness/cogency

Shall we believe in God?

In this excerpt from Pensees, Blaise Pascal gives us his argument that we ought to believe in God.

Let's examine Pascal's argument, starting with the important passages where he lays it out.

Blaise Pascal
Close

Not decided by epistemic reasons

"Either God is, or he is not. But which side shall we incline towards? Reason cannot settle anything here. There is an infinite chaos that separates us. A game is being played at the far end of this infinite distance: the coin will come down heads or tails. How will you bet? Reason will not enable you to decide either way, or rule out either alternative." (p. 60)

An issue of your happiness

"Since you must choose, let us see which is the least profitable option. You have two things to lose, the true and the good, and two things to stake, your reason and your will, your knowledge and your happiness. Your nature has two things to avoid, error and wretchedness. Since a choice must necessarily be made, your reason is no more offended by choosing one rather than the other. But your happiness? Let us weigh up the gain and the loss in choosing heads. that God exists... If you win, you win everything, and if you lose, you lose nothing. So bet that he exists, without hesitation." (p. 60)

Extracting Pascal's argument

Pascal's reason for belief in God is a pragmatic one. While pragmatic reasons don’t necessarily concern the truth of some claim, it doesn’t mean that they can’t be the subject of rigorous argument. And a rigorous argument for believing in God is just what Pascal tries to provide.

  1. If you believe in God, you have the chance for eternal happiness if it turns out he does exist.

  2. If you don’t believe in God, you risk eternal damnation if it turns out he does exist.

  3. The possible reward for belief is huge, and the risk for non-belief is devastatingly high.

  4. (Implicit)If the potential reward for an action is very high, you ought to perform that action.

  5. So, you should believe in God.

Determining the support relation

Cost/benefit analysis

  • An individual is faced with a choice between A and B.

  • The individual tallies up the pros and cons of both A and B.

  • The individual adjusts the lists above to factor in the significance of the pros and cons for A and B.

  • The individual compares the weighted lists.

  • Whichever choice comes out ahead is the one the individual decides to carry out.

Cost/benefit analysis is a basic form of human reasoning. It is something that we perform all the time. Let’s call the pros and cons of various choices outcomes of that choice.

Measuring significance

It isn’t very valuable to just list out the pros and cons and add up the number of each, because not all outcomes are of the same value.

There are two ways that significance can factor in:

Some outcomes are better or worse than others.

If I’m looking at two different jobs and both offer 2 perks:

  • Job A offers free coffee and a parking spot.
  • Job B offers dental coverage and paid vacation time
I’m obviously going to choose job B. I weight it's perks more highly.

Some outcomes are more likely than others.

When we’re deciding what to do, we don’t always know what will result from certain choices because we don’t know exactly how the world is laid out.

So we want to consider all sorts of possible outcomes.

But we can make educated guesses about how likely certain outcomes are, and the more likely ones should count for more in our decision making.

What we need is a way of making these ideas about the significance of outcomes more precise, so that we can better determine what the right choice is.

Decision theory is an attempt to do just this.

Decision Matrices

In the rows, we list all the possible actions among which we are attempting to choose.

In the columns, we list all the possible states of the world among which we are uncertain which is actually the case.

Each cell is an outcome, and we place utility values for each outcome in the relevant cell.

Example matrix

State 1 (S1) State 2 (S2)
Action 1 (A1) Val(A1S1) Val(A1S2)
Action 2 (A2) Val(A2S1) Val(A2S2)

Expected utility

We can then use this matrix to determine which of the actions before us is the best.

To do this, we need to assign probabilities to the various states of affairs. The value we assign to a state of affairs represents how likely we think it is that that state of affairs will obtain.

The expected utility is a numerical representation of how good of a choice that action is compared to the other options.

EU(A1) = Val(A1S1) × P(S1) + Val(A1S2) × P(S2) + ...

After calculating the expected value for each of the actions we have to choose from, the action that gets the highest result is the one that we have most pragmatic reason to choose.

Do we have pragmatic reason to believe that God exists?

Let’s apply these tools to the choice of whether or not to believe in God. Below is how Pascal would set up the decision matrix.

Pascal's matrix

God exists God doesn’t exist
Believe in God  + ∞ finite
Don’t believe in God  − ∞ finite

Let’s assume that we have no epistemic reason one way or the other regarding belief in God. That means that the probability of each state of affairs is split 50/50. Thus, we calculate the expected value of each option:

Believe

EU(Believe) = Val(Believe,God) × P(God) + Val(Believe,no-God) × P(no-God)
= + ∞ × 0.5 + finite × 0.5
= + ∞

Don't believe

EU(not-Believe) = Val(not-Believe,God) × P(God) + Val(not-Believe,no-God) × P(no-God)
= - ∞ × 0.5 + finite × 0.5
= - ∞

Thus, since positive infinity is vastly better than negative infinity, this setup suggests we have good pragmatic reason to believe in God.

Objections

While the argument is pretty straightforward, there are some complications. Let’s look at some objections to the argument that are usually raised. The first three are common, but Pascal can offer an answer to them. The last two are a bit more serious.

It’s not enough for someone just to believe in God for them to get into Heaven

This may be true, but it doesn’t really impact Pascal’s argument. After all, it’s still necessary for someone to get into Heaven that they believe in God. So, you still have reason to believe, even if you must do other stuff as well.

One can’t just choose to believe something.

This is also true, but if you recognize that you have pragmatic reason to believe, you can do certain things to help you develop the belief in God. For instance, you can go to church and surround yourself with other believers. If you work at it, you will likely engender a belief in God.

Don’t we also have the choice of withholding belief entirely?

It is true that for many claims it is a genuine third option to withhold belief. For instance, I neither believe nor disbelieve that there are an even number of particles in the universe.

But in the case of belief in God, withholding belief is equivalent to disbelief. At least according to the model Pascal is working with, agnostics suffer the same fate as atheists.

We can’t know God's will.

Pascal already admitted that epistemic reason can't tell us much of anything about God. Maybe God punishes those who believe based on pragmatic arguments. That is, he only accepts those who come to believe in him for the right reasons. If so, then the values we placed in the decision matrix might not be justified.

What about other gods?

This possibility of other gods is not in and of itself problematic for Pascal’s argument because the existence of different religions doesn’t change the values we put in the boxes of our decision matrix.

But it does suggest that our matrix might be incomplete.

Imagine the possibility “Anti-God” exists. Anti-God punishes anyone who believes in God by sending them to Hell and accepts anyone who doesn’t believe in God into Heaven. If we consider this possibility, our decision matix should look like:

Expanded matrix

God exists no God exists Anti-God exists
Believe in God  + ∞ finite  − ∞
Don’t believe in God  − ∞ finite  + ∞

And our new expected values look like:

Believe

EU(Believe) = Val(Believe,God) × P(God) + Val(Believe,no-God) × P(no-God) + Val(Believe,anti-God) × P(anti-God)
= + ∞ × 0.33 + finite × 0.33 - ∞ × 0.33
= finite

Don't believe

EU(not-Believe) = Val(not-Believe,God) × P(God) + Val(not-Believe,no-God) × P(no-God) + Val(not-Believe,anti-God) × P(anti-God)
= - ∞ × 0.33 + finite × 0.33 + ∞ × 0.33
= finite

So now it appears that both options are equally beneficial. The upshot is that so long as the existence of Anti-God is a genuine possibility, we don’t really have a pragmatic reason for believing in God.

Pascal on wagering lives

"You would be foolish, since you are forced to play, not to risk your life to gain three lives in a game where there is equal chance of losing and winning. But there is an eternity of life and happiness. This being so, in a game where there were an infinity of chances and only one in your favor, you would still be right to wager one life in order to gain two; and you would be making the wrong choice, given that you were obliged to play, if you refused to bet one life against three in a game where there were an infinity of chances and only one in your favor, if the prize were an infinity of infinitely happy life. But the prize here is an infinity of infinitely happy life, one chance of winning against a finite number of chances of losing, and what you are staking is finite."
