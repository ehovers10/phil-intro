Assessing arguments

  • Deductive
  • Inductive

Deductive arguments

Validity

Validity measures the support relation

Deductive arguments purport to give conclusive reason to believe the conclusion.

If the argument is a good one, the conclusion follows directly from the premises.

We say that a deductive argument is valid just in case if the premises are all true, then the conclusion must be true.

Soundness

Soundness tracks the truth of the premises

But not all valid arguments have true premises.

If a valid argument also has true premises, we say that it is sound.

If we have a sound argument, we know for certain that the conclusion is true.

Examples

Invalid argument

  1. All penguins are birds. (True)

  2. Some birds fly. (True)

  3. Therefore , penguins fly. (False)

Valid but unsound argument

  1. Either Eli Manning is a running back or dolphins are fish. (False)

  2. Dolphins are not fish. (True)

  3. So , Eli Manning is a running back. (False)

Inductive arguments

Strength

Strength (measures the support relation)

Inductive arguments purport to give reason to believe that the conclusion is probable.

The better the argument is, the more probable the conclusion becomes.

We say that an inductive argument is strong just in case if the premises are all true, then the conclusion is very likely to be true.

Cogency

Cogency (measures the truth of the premises)

But not all strong arguments have true premises.

If a strong argument also has true premises, we say that it is cogent.

If we have a cogent argument, we know that the conclusion very probably true.

Examples

Weak argument

  1. I know a lawyer who is a total liar. (True)

  2. Bill is also a lawyer. (True)

  3. Consequently , Bill is probably a liar. (False)

Strong but not cogent argument

  1. 89% of male college basketball players are over 6 feet tall. (False)

  2. Bill is a college basketball player. (True)

  3. Thus , Bill is probably over 6 feet tall. (True)
